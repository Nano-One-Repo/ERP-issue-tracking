Pattern Summary — Since Project Start
Looking across the full project lifecycle — from initial ERP scope decisions through Phase 1 go-live — a set of deeper structural patterns have repeated consistently at every stage.
01
Scope was never rightsized
The ERP was implemented at near full-enterprise scope for a company that had already pivoted away from in-house manufacturing. Modules like Projects, WIP, and Inventory — which require significant internal operational capacity to run — were included without validating whether Nano One had the bandwidth or the processes to support them. Projects went live at 0%. Inventory is still at 0%.
02
PM accountability gap — start to finish
The signed SOW established clear PM obligations: own the project plan, communicate status, manage Nano One resources, attend and prepare for weekly calls. None of these were consistently delivered. The pattern held from the earliest phases through go-live week — required meetings not held until too late, stakeholders not invited, documentation not maintained.
03
Critical gaps discovered too late — repeatedly
Lite licence limitations (cannot change currency on a PR, cannot attach PDFs) were discovered at go-live. Static table prep was missed until very late. UoM was at 0% at go-live. Vendor master data ownership was unresolved days before cutover. In each case, the gap existed long before it was surfaced — and the surfacing was reactive, not through any PM-led process.
04
ESS / vendor delivery shortfalls
ESS (the implementation vendor) failed to communicate the Lite licence limitations clearly. SOW expectations were not communicated to Nano One stakeholders. SyteLine environments showed active session errors during critical testing windows. PO Change Order report modifications remained outstanding through go-live. The project relied on the PM to hold ESS accountable — and the PM was not doing so.
05
Internal team absorbed the deficit
In the absence of PM-led coordination, Nano One staff — Blake, Maryam, Gary, Ian, Wei and others — informally took on coordination, data prep, and issue triage that should have been PM-owned or vendor-owned. This created invisible workload, unclear accountability, and a false sense of progress since work was getting done but without formal sign-off or tracking.
06
Decision-making deferred until crisis
Who handles PO receiving at each site? Who assigns expense accounts to PRs? Who is the DRI for vendor data cleanup? These questions existed throughout the project but were deferred — sometimes until the go/no-go decision itself. The go-live proceeded with several of these still unresolved. The decision log was never formally maintained.
07
Training misalignment — theory vs real process
Training was delivered but not aligned to actual configured workflows. Core concepts (PR vs PO, approval routing, WIP) remained unclear to users who were expected to be live in the system. The Roles End-to-End process document existed but was not used as a training alignment tool. End-user sign-off was not consistently obtained before go-live.
08
No formal post-go-live safety net
Post-implementation audit, open issue resolution, data integrity verification, and end-user efficiency measurement are all sitting at 0% with no assigned owners and no confirmed start dates. The project went live without a hypercare plan. The first financial month-end close in SyteLine — a high-risk event for any new ERP deployment — has no preparation underway.
The root cause across all eight patterns is the same: the project lacked a functioning PM to translate the SOW into execution. Everything else is a downstream consequence.
